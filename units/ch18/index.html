Introduction: What is Magnetic Field?

Think about these questions:
  • Why does static electricity make your hair stand up?
  • Why does lightning strike during storms?
  • How does a capacitor store charge in an electronic circuit?

Electric fields explain how charged objects interact without touching - analogous to how gravitational fields explain mass interactions. Every charged object creates an electric field in the space around it. When another charge enters this field, it experiences a force.

Paper 4 Examination Focus: Electric fields questions frequently combine with capacitor topics (Unit 19). Be prepared to calculate forces, field strengths, and potentials using the formulas.

This unit develops the beautiful parallel between electric fields and gravitational fields (Unit 13). The mathematical structures are identical - same inverse-square law, same concept of potential - but the source is charge instead of mass.

1. Coulomb's Law of Electrostatics

Definition: Coulomb's Law
The electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of their separation.
Coulomb's Law:
\[ F = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2} = k\frac{Q_1 Q_2}{r^2} \]
where \( k = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2 \, \text{C}^{-2} \) and \( \varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F m}^{-1} \)

1.1 Key Properties

  • Attractive if \( Q_1 Q_2 < 0 \) (opposite charges)
  • Repulsive if \( Q_1 Q_2 > 0 \) (same charges)
  • Acts along the line joining the two charges
  • Inverse square law: \( F \propto \dfrac{1}{r^2} \)
  • Same mathematical form as Newton's Law of Gravitation

1.2 Vector Nature

Electrostatic force is a vector quantity. When finding the net force on a charge due to multiple other charges, you must consider direction - use parallelogram law or vector component method.

Common Mistake: Students often simply add the magnitudes of forces when there are multiple charges. This is wrong - you must consider whether each force is attractive or repulsive, find the vector sum, and give a direction.

2. Electric Field Strength (E)

Definition: Electric Field Strength
Electric field strength at a point is defined as the electrostatic force per unit positive charge placed at that point.
Electric Field Strength:
\[ E = \frac{F}{q} = \frac{kQ}{r^2} \]
Unit: N C-1 or V m-1 (equivalent)

2.1 Direction of Electric Field

  • Electric field strength is a vector
  • Direction: the same direction as the force on a positive test charge
  • Away from positive source charge
  • Toward negative source charge

2.2 Point Charge Formula

For a point charge Q at distance r:

\[ E = \frac{kQ}{r^2} \]
Field radiates outward for positive Q, inward for negative Q
Test Charge Convention: In E = F/q, the charge q is a small positive test charge placed to probe the field. In E = kQ/r2, the charge Q is the source charge creating the field.

2.3 Formula Selection Guide

  • Point charge field: \( E = \dfrac{kQ}{r^2} \)
  • Uniform field (parallel plates): \( E = \dfrac{V}{d} \)
  • From force and charge: \( E = \dfrac{F}{q} \)

3. Electric Field Patterns

Electric field lines provide a visual representation of electric fields. Key rules:

  • Arrows point in the direction of force on a positive test charge
  • Lines start on positive charges, end on negative charges
  • Line density is proportional to field strength (denser lines = stronger field)
  • Lines never cross (field has only one direction at any point)

3.2 Equipotential Lines

Definition: Equipotential
An equipotential is a line or surface joining points of equal electric potential.
Key Property: Equipotential lines are always perpendicular to electric field lines.

In a uniform field, equipotentials are parallel lines perpendicular to field lines.

In a radial field, equipotentials are concentric circles (2D) or spheres (3D).

4. Electric Potential (V) and Potential Energy

Definition: Electric Potential
Electric potential at a point is defined as the work done per unit positive charge in bringing a small positive test charge from infinity to that point.
Electric Potential:
\[ V = \frac{W}{q} = \frac{kQ}{r} \]
Unit: volt (V) = JC-1
Definition: Electric Potential Energy
The electric potential energy of a charge at a point is the work done in bringing that charge from infinity to that point.
Electric Potential Energy:
\[ E_p = qV = \frac{kQq}{r} \]
For point charge: potential V = 0 at r = infinity

4.1 Key Points

  • Electric potential is a scalar quantity (add algebraically)
  • For multiple charges: \( V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 \)
  • Positive charge: potential positive, decreases toward zero at infinity
  • Negative charge: potential negative, increases toward zero at infinity
Scalar Addition: Unlike forces (vectors), electric potentials add like ordinary numbers. Just add the values, keeping the sign.

4.2 Potential Gradient

The relationship between electric field strength and potential gradient:

\[ E = -\frac{\Delta V}{\Delta d} \]
The negative sign indicates E points toward decreasing potential

5. Uniform Electric Field (Parallel Plates)

Between two parallel conducting plates with a potential difference V and separation d, the field is uniform (same magnitude and direction everywhere).

Uniform Field Strength:
\[ E = \frac{V}{d} \]
Verify: N C-1 = V m-1

5.1 Force on Charged Particle

\[ F = qE = q\frac{V}{d} \]
Direction: toward plate of opposite sign

5.2 Particle Deflection

  • Force is constant (uniform field)
  • Trajectory is parabolic (like projectile motion)
  • Gravity is usually negligible (electric force >> gravitational force for electrons)
  • Acceleration: \( a = \dfrac{F}{m} = \dfrac{qV}{md} \)
Applications: CRO oscilloscopes (electron beam deflection), inkjet printers, electrostatic precipitators, particle accelerators.
