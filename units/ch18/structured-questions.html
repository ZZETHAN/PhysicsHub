P4 Style Coulomb's Law
[6 marks]

(a) State Coulomb's law for the electrostatic force between two point charges. [2]

(b) Two small spheres, X and Y, carry charges of +2.0 micro coulomb and -3.0 micro coulomb respectively. The spheres are 0.15 m apart.

Calculate the magnitude of the electrostatic force acting on X. [3]

(c) State and explain whether the force on X is attractive or repulsive. [1]

[Image Placeholder] Fig 18.1: Two point charges X and Y with force arrows
Diagram showing the arrangement of charges X and Y
Mark Scheme
Mark distribution: C1 = correct quantitative expression | C2 = correct calculation | A1 = final answer with unit | B1 = explanatory point

(a) [2 marks]

C1 \( F = \dfrac{kQ_1 Q_2}{r^2} \) where \( k = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \) or \( k = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N m}^2 \, \text{C}^{-2} \)
B1 Force is inversely proportional to square of separation / acts along line joining charges

(b) [3 marks]

C1 Substituting: \( F = \dfrac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(2.0 \times 10^{-6})(3.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.15)^2} \)
C1 = 2.4 N (accept 2.39 N)

(c) [1 mark]

B1 Attractive, because charges are opposite signs (positive and negative)
P4 Style Electric Field
[8 marks]

(a) Define electric field strength at a point in an electric field. [2]

(b) A point charge Q = +4.0 micro coulomb produces an electric field.

Calculate the electric field strength at a point 0.20 m from the charge. [3]

(c) On Fig. 1, draw the electric field pattern around a single positive point charge. Show at least three field lines and indicate the direction of the field. [3]

[Image Placeholder] Fig 18.2: Single positive point charge Q
Draw electric field pattern around charge Q
Mark Scheme
Mark distribution: B marks = fundamental physics points | C marks = calculations | A marks = final answer

(a) [2 marks]

B1 Force per unit positive charge / \( E = F/q \) where q is a small positive test charge
B1 Direction: in direction of force on positive charge

(b) [3 marks]

C1 \( E = \dfrac{kQ}{r^2} = \dfrac{(8.99 \times 10^9)(4.0 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.20)^2} \)
A1 = \( 9.0 \times 10^5 \, \text{N C}^{-1} \) (or 900 kN C-1)

(c) [3 marks]

B1 Radial field lines emanating FROM the positive charge
B1 Arrow heads pointing AWAY from charge (radially outward)
B1 At least 3 lines, evenly distributed (360 degrees or representative sector)
P4 Style Potential
[6 marks]

(a) Define electric potential at a point in an electric field. [2]

(b) The potential at a point P is 600 V. A charge of 2.0 micro coulomb is placed at P.

Calculate the electric potential energy of the charge at this point. [2]

(c) Explain why the potential is taken as zero at infinity. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark distribution: B = conceptual | C = calculation

(a) [2 marks]

B1 Work done per unit charge in bringing a small positive test charge from infinity to the point / \( V = W/q \)
B1 Or: potential energy per unit charge

(b) [2 marks]

C1 \( E_p = qV = (2.0 \times 10^{-6})(600) \)
A1 = \( 1.2 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{J} \) (or 1.2 mJ)

(c) [2 marks]

B1 At infinity, the field strength is zero (no interaction between charges)
B1 Therefore no work is done in bringing a charge from infinity, so potential energy (and hence V) = 0
P4 Style Uniform Field
[8 marks]

(a) Two parallel conducting plates are 0.050 m apart. The potential difference between the plates is 240 V.

Calculate the electric field strength between the plates, stating the appropriate unit. [2]

(b) An electron is placed at the midpoint between the plates.

Calculate the force acting on the electron. [2]

(c) Describe the motion of the electron after release, explaining how this compares to the motion of a particle in a gravitational field. [4]

[Image Placeholder] Fig 18.3: Parallel plates with electron at midpoint
Diagram showing parallel plates with electron position
Mark Scheme
Mark distribution: C = calculation | B = physics explanation

(a) [2 marks]

C1 \( E = V/d = 240 / 0.050 \)
A1 = 4800 V m-1 (or 4.8 kV m-1)

(b) [2 marks]

C1 \( F = eE = (1.6 \times 10^{-19})(4800) \)
A1 = \( 7.7 \times 10^{-16} \, \text{N} \) (toward positive plate)

(c) [4 marks]

B1 Electron accelerates toward positive plate (opposite to field direction)
B1 Motion is similar to particle in gravitational field: constant acceleration
B1 Difference: electron accelerates toward lower potential (negative charges move to increase potential energy), whereas mass moves toward lower gravitational potential
B1 Unlike gravity, electric force can be attractive OR repulsive
P4 Style Potential Gradient
[5 marks]

(a) State the relationship between electric field strength and potential gradient. [1]

(b) In a uniform electric field, the potential changes from 100 V at one plate to 60 V at the other plate. The plate separation is 0.020 m.

Calculate the magnitude of the electric field strength between the plates. [2]

(c) Explain why electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. [2]

[Image Placeholder] Fig 18.4: Equipotential lines and field lines between plates
Showing perpendicular relationship
Mark Scheme
Mark distribution: B = conceptual | C = calculation

(a) [1 mark]

B1 \( E = -\dfrac{\Delta V}{\Delta d} \) or field strength equals negative potential gradient

(b) [2 marks]

C1 \( E = \dfrac{\Delta V}{\Delta d} = \dfrac{100 - 60}{0.020} = \dfrac{40}{0.020} \)
A1 = 2000 V m-1

(c) [2 marks]

B1 No work is done moving along an equipotential (by definition)
B1 Since work done = Fd cos theta, and F is along field line, for zero work cos theta must = 0, so field line is perpendicular to equipotential
Exam Tips:
  • Always state the direction of forces and fields
  • Remember: electric field strength E = F/q for ANY field, not just point charges
  • Potential V is a scalar quantity - add algebraically
  • In uniform fields: E = V/d and field points from high to low potential
  • The negative sign in E = -dV/dx shows E points toward decreasing potential
