Introduction: What is Wave?

Think about these motions:
  • 🕐 The back-and-forth motion of a clock pendulum
  • 🎵 The vibration of a guitar string when plucked
  • 🌳 The swaying of tree branches in the wind
  • 🐝 The fluttering of a bee's wings

Oscillation is a repeating back-and-forth motion between two limits. It's one of the most common types of mechanical motion in nature.

In this unit, we will study a special type of oscillation called Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM), where the motion follows a precise mathematical pattern.

1. Observing Oscillations

Common Features of Oscillations:
  • Periodicity: The motion repeats in a regular cycle
  • Symmetry: Motion is symmetric about a central equilibrium position
  • Restoring force: A force always acts to return the object to equilibrium

1.1 Examples of Oscillating Systems

a) A Simple Pendulum

A mass on a string swinging back and forth about a fixed point. At the bottom of the swing, the mass is at its equilibrium position.

b) A Mass-Spring System

A mass attached to a spring oscillates back and forth when displaced from its equilibrium position. When the spring is not deformed, the net force on the mass is zero.

Equilibrium Position (O): The position where the oscillating object rests when not disturbed. At this point:
  • The net force on the object is zero
  • The object is often moving at maximum speed

c) A Loudspeaker Cone

The cone of a loudspeaker vibrates back and forth when driven by an electrical signal, creating sound waves.

2. Describing Simple Harmonic Motion

Definition of Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM):

A body executes simple harmonic motion if:
  1. Its acceleration is directly proportional to its displacement from its equilibrium position
  2. Its acceleration is in the opposite direction to its displacement (acceleration is always towards the equilibrium position)
Restoring Force:

A restoring force acts to return the mass to its equilibrium position. The restoring force F is directly proportional to the displacement x of the mass from its equilibrium position and is directed towards that point.

2.1 Key Quantities in SHM

a) Amplitude (A)

Amplitude for s.h.m.: The maximum displacement from the equilibrium position. Amplitude is a scalar quantity and remains constant in undamped SHM.

Amplitude: \(A = x_{\text{max}}\)
Unit: metres (m)

b) Displacement (x)

Displacement for s.h.m.: The distance moved by object in a given direction from the equilibrium position.

Key Distinction:
  • Displacement is a vector, while amplitude is a scalar
  • The maximum displacement equals the amplitude
  • In a given oscillation, the oscillator's displacement varies with time, but the amplitude remains constant

c) Period (T) and Frequency (f)

Period for s.h.m.: Time taken for one complete oscillation.

Period: \(T = \frac{1}{f}\)

Frequency: \(f = \frac{1}{T}\)

Angular Frequency: \(\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T}\)

Units: \(T\) in seconds (s), \(f\) in hertz (Hz), \(\omega\) in rad s⁻¹

d) Phase Difference

In SHM, displacement, velocity, and acceleration all vary sinusoidally but are out of phase with each other:

  • Velocity leads displacement by \(90°\) or \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) rad
  • Acceleration is \(180°\) or \(\pi\) rad out of phase with displacement

3. Equations of Simple Harmonic Motion

3.1 Displacement Equation

The displacement of an object in SHM varies sinusoidally with time:

Displacement:

\(x = A \cos(\omega t)\) or \(x = A \sin(\omega t)\)

Where:
  • \(x\) = displacement at time \(t\)
  • \(A\) = amplitude
  • \(\omega\) = angular frequency
  • \(t\) = time

3.2 Velocity Equation

Velocity is the rate of change of displacement:

Velocity:

\(v = \pm \omega \sqrt{A^2 - x^2}\)

Or in terms of time:
\(v = -A\omega \sin(\omega t)\)

Maximum velocity occurs at equilibrium (\(x = 0\)):
\(v_{\text{max}} = A\omega\)

3.3 Acceleration Equation

Acceleration is proportional to displacement but in the opposite direction:

Acceleration:

\(a = -\omega^2 x\)

Or in terms of time:
\(a = -A\omega^2 \cos(\omega t)\)

Maximum acceleration occurs at maximum displacement:
\(a_{\text{max}} = A\omega^2\)

3.4 Period Formulas for Specific Systems

a) Mass-Spring System

Period of mass-spring system:

\[T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}\]

Where:
  • \(m\) = mass (kg)
  • \(k\) = spring constant (N m⁻¹)

b) Simple Pendulum (small angles)

Period of simple pendulum:

\[T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\]

Where:
  • \(L\) = length of pendulum (m)
  • \(g\) = acceleration due to gravity (m s⁻²)

Note: This formula is valid only for small angles (\(\theta < 10°\))

4. Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

4.1 Energy Transformations

In SHM, energy continuously transforms between kinetic and potential forms:

  • At maximum displacement (A or -A): All energy is potential, kinetic energy is zero
  • At equilibrium (x = 0): All energy is kinetic, potential energy is zero
  • Between extremes: Energy is a mixture of both forms

4.2 Kinetic Energy

Kinetic Energy:

\[E_k = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(A^2 - x^2)\]

Maximum KE (at \(x = 0\)):
\(E_{k,\text{max}} = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 = \frac{1}{2}m v_{\text{max}}^2\)

4.3 Potential Energy

Potential Energy:

\[E_p = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\]

Maximum PE (at \(x = A\)):
\(E_{p,\text{max}} = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2\)

4.4 Total Mechanical Energy

Total Energy:

\[E_{\text{total}} = E_k + E_p = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2\]

Key Point: Total mechanical energy is constant (assuming no damping)
Important Relationship:
  • Total energy is proportional to amplitude squared: \(E \propto A^2\)
  • If amplitude doubles, total energy increases by factor of 4

5. Damped Oscillations

Damping: The reduction of the amplitude of an oscillating system due to loss of energy caused by dissipative forces.

5.1 Free vs. Damped Oscillations

Feature Free Oscillation (Ideal) Damped Oscillation (Real)
Amplitude Constant Decreases over time
Total Energy Conserved Decreases over time
Period/Frequency Constant Slightly increased period

5.2 Types of Damping

  • Light damping: System oscillates with gradually decreasing amplitude
  • Critical damping: System returns to equilibrium in shortest possible time without oscillating
  • Heavy damping: System returns to equilibrium slowly without oscillating

5.3 Practical Examples of Damping

  • Shock absorbers in cars
  • Door closers
  • Swings in air

6. Resonance

6.1 Forced Oscillations

When a periodic external force is applied to an oscillating system:

  • The system vibrates at the driving frequency (not its natural frequency)
  • The steady-state frequency equals the forcing frequency
  • Examples: Bus engine vibrations, washing machine drums, bridges with rhythmic marching
Natural Frequency (\(f_0\)): The frequency at which body will vibrate when there is no resultant external resistive force acting on it.

Forcing Frequency (\(f\)): The frequency at which object is made to vibrate/oscillate.

6.2 Resonance

Resonance: Occurs when the driving frequency equals the natural frequency of the system, resulting in maximum amplitude of oscillation.
Condition for Resonance:

\(f = f_0\) or \(\omega = \omega_0\)

At resonance, the amplitude reaches its maximum value.

6.3 Examples of Resonance

  • Bridge collapse: Soldiers marching in step can cause resonance in bridges
  • Breaking a wine glass: Sound waves at the right frequency
  • Microwave ovens: Radiation frequency matches water molecule natural frequency
  • Radio/TV tuning: Circuit resonant frequency matches broadcast frequency
  • MRI machines: Nuclear magnetic resonance

6.4 Reducing Resonance Effects

  • Add damping to absorb energy
  • Change the natural frequency of the system
  • Avoid operating at resonant frequency
