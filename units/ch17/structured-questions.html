Oscillations - Past Exam Questions with Mark Schemes

P4 Style SHM Definition
[8 marks]

(a) State the two conditions that must be satisfied for a body to execute simple harmonic motion. [2]

(b) A small ball moves from rest down an inclined plane RA and then rises up plane LA. The two planes have the same gradient and meet at their lower edges. The ball then moves down plane LA and rises up plane RA to its original height. The motion repeats itself.

State and explain whether the motion of the ball is simple harmonic. [3]

(c) A trolley of mass 0.80 kg is attached to two identical springs, each with spring constant \(k = 25\) N m⁻¹, arranged horizontally. The trolley is displaced 5.0 cm from equilibrium and released.

Calculate:

  • the period of oscillation, [2]
  • the maximum speed of the trolley. [1]
Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
(a) - SHM Conditions [2 marks]
B1
Acceleration is directly proportional to displacement from equilibrium
B1
Acceleration is directed towards equilibrium position / opposite to displacement
(b) - Motion Analysis [3 marks]
B1
Motion is NOT simple harmonic
B1
Acceleration is constant (not proportional to displacement)
B1
Acceleration does not change direction continuously towards equilibrium
(c) - Calculations [3 marks]
M1
Effective spring constant = \(2k = 50\) N m⁻¹
M1
\(T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k_{\text{eff}}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.80/50}\)
A1
Period = 0.79 s (accept 0.795 s)
M1
\(v_{\text{max}} = A\omega = 0.05 \times \sqrt{50/0.80}\)
A1
Maximum speed = 0.40 m s⁻¹
Examiner Tip: For two springs in parallel, the effective spring constant is the sum of individual constants. Always check whether the question describes series or parallel arrangement.
P4 Style Energy in SHM
[10 marks]

(a) A mass of 0.20 kg is vibrating with simple harmonic motion of amplitude 4.0 cm and period 0.50 s.

Calculate:

  • the total energy of the oscillation, [2]
  • the potential energy when the displacement is 2.0 cm, [2]
  • the kinetic energy at the same displacement. [1]

(b) The amplitude of the oscillation is now increased to 6.0 cm without changing the period.

State, with a reason, what happens to:

  • the total energy of the system, [2]
  • the maximum speed of the mass. [3]
Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
(a) - Energy Calculations [5 marks]
M1
\(\omega = 2\pi/T = 2\pi/0.50 = 12.6\) rad s⁻¹
M1
\(E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2 = 0.5 \times 0.20 \times (12.6)^2 \times (0.04)^2\)
A1
Total energy = 2.53 × 10⁻² J (accept 2.5 × 10⁻² J)
M1
\(E_p = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\) with \(x = 0.02\) m
A1
PE = 6.3 × 10⁻³ J (accept 6.4 × 10⁻³ J)
M1
\(E_k = E_{\text{total}} - E_p\)
A1
KE = 1.9 × 10⁻² J (accept 1.89 × 10⁻² J)
(b) - Amplitude Change Analysis [5 marks]
B1
Total energy increases (by factor of 9/4 or 2.25)
B1
Reason: \(E \propto A^2\), so if amplitude increases by factor of 1.5, energy increases by 2.25
B1
Maximum speed increases (by factor of 1.5)
M1
\(v_{\text{max}} = A\omega\) and \(\omega\) is constant
B1
Therefore \(v_{\text{max}} \propto A\), so speed increases by same factor as amplitude
Examiner Tip: Remember the key relationships: \(E \propto A^2\) but \(v_{\text{max}} \propto A\). Total energy depends on amplitude squared, while maximum velocity depends linearly on amplitude.
P4 Style Pendulum & Resonance
[12 marks]

(a) A simple pendulum has a period of 2.0 s on Earth where \(g = 9.81\) m s⁻².

Calculate:

  • the length of the pendulum, [2]
  • the period of the same pendulum on the Moon where \(g = 1.62\) m s⁻². [2]

(b) Explain why the period of a simple pendulum is independent of the mass of the bob. [2]

(c) A child on a swing is an example of a pendulum system. Describe and explain how the child can increase the amplitude of the swing by pumping their legs. [3]

(d) State one everyday example of resonance and explain how it can be useful or dangerous. [3]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
(a) - Pendulum Calculations [4 marks]
M1
Rearranging \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\) to give \(L = gT^2/4\pi^2\)
A1
\(L = 9.81 \times (2.0)^2 / 4\pi^2 = 0.99\) m (accept 1.0 m)
M1
\(T_{\text{moon}} = T_{\text{earth}} \times \sqrt{g_{\text{earth}}/g_{\text{moon}}}\)
A1
\(T_{\text{moon}} = 2.0 \times \sqrt{9.81/1.62} = 4.9\) s (accept 5.0 s)
(b) - Mass Independence [2 marks]
B1
Restoring force \(F = mg\sin\theta \propto m\)
B1
Acceleration \(a = F/m = g\sin\theta\) is independent of mass
(c) - Pumping Action [3 marks]>
B1
Child pumps legs at natural frequency of swing
B1
Energy is transferred to the system
B1
Resonance occurs, amplitude increases
(d) - Resonance Example [3 marks]>
B1
Valid example given (e.g., microwave oven, bridge collapse, radio tuning)
B1
Explanation of driving frequency matching natural frequency
B1
Explanation of usefulness/danger
Examiner Tip: When explaining why pendulum period is independent of mass, focus on the fact that while the restoring force depends on mass, the acceleration does not (since F = ma). This is similar to why all objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum.
💡 Exam Tips for Oscillations:
  • Always draw a clear distinction between displacement, amplitude, and distance traveled
  • Remember that acceleration is always directed towards equilibrium, opposite to displacement
  • For pendulum calculations, ensure angles are small (< 10°) for SHM approximation
  • Energy questions: total energy ∝ A², maximum speed ∝ A, maximum acceleration ∝ A
  • Resonance: maximum amplitude occurs when driving frequency equals natural frequency
