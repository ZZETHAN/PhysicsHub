Introduction: The Ideal Gas

Think about these questions:
  • Why does a balloon expand when heated?
  • Why does a car tire pressure increase on a hot day?
  • How can we predict the behavior of gases under different conditions?
  • What is the relationship between temperature and molecular motion?

In this unit, we connect the macroscopic properties of gases (pressure, volume, temperature) to the microscopic motion of molecules. This connection is one of the triumphs of classical physics.

Connection to Unit 14: In Temperature, we learned about thermal equilibrium and energy transfer. In this unit, we discover that temperature is directly proportional to the average kinetic energy of molecules.
Paper 4 Examination Focus: Expect calculations using the ideal gas equation \(pV = nRT\) or \(pV = NkT\). Be able to derive and use the molecular kinetic energy equation. Understand the assumptions of the kinetic theory model.

1. The Gas Laws

The Experimental Basis: Before developing a theoretical model, scientists discovered three empirical laws describing gas behavior at constant temperature, pressure, or volume.

1.1 Boyle's Law (Constant Temperature)

Boyle's Law

For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, the pressure is inversely proportional to the volume.

\[p \propto \frac{1}{V} \quad \text{or} \quad pV = \text{constant}\]

Alternative form: \(p_1V_1 = p_2V_2\)

[插图占位符 - Boyle's Law 示意图：p-V 图和 p-1/V 图]

Physical interpretation: When volume decreases, molecules collide with container walls more frequently, increasing pressure.

1.2 Charles's Law (Constant Pressure)

Charles's Law

For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.

\[V \propto T \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{V}{T} = \text{constant}\]

Note: Temperature must be in Kelvin!

1.3 The Pressure Law (Constant Volume)

The Pressure Law (Gay-Lussac's Law)

For a fixed mass of gas at constant volume, the pressure is directly proportional to the absolute temperature.

\[p \propto T \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{p}{T} = \text{constant}\]
Common Mistake: Always use absolute temperature (Kelvin) in gas law calculations. To convert: \(T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273\)

2. The Ideal Gas Equation

Combining the Laws: Boyle's, Charles's, and the Pressure Law can be combined into a single equation relating all three variables.
The Ideal Gas Equation (Molar Form)
\[pV = nRT\]

where:

  • \(p\) = pressure (Pa)
  • \(V\) = volume (m³)
  • \(n\) = number of moles (mol)
  • \(R\) = molar gas constant = 8.31 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹
  • \(T\) = absolute temperature (K)
The Ideal Gas Equation (Molecular Form)
\[pV = NkT\]

where:

  • \(N\) = number of molecules
  • \(k\) = Boltzmann constant = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹

Relationship: \(R = N_Ak\) where \(N_A\) is the Avogadro constant.

2.1 The Ideal Gas

An ideal gas is a theoretical model that obeys the ideal gas equation exactly. Real gases approximate ideal gas behavior at:

  • High temperatures - molecules move fast, intermolecular forces negligible
  • Low pressures - molecules far apart, volume of molecules negligible compared to container
Key Constants to Remember:
  • \(R = 8.31 \text{ J mol}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}\)
  • \(k = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \text{ J K}^{-1}\)
  • \(N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}\)

3. Kinetic Theory of Gases

The Microscopic View: Instead of treating gas as a continuous fluid, kinetic theory models it as a collection of molecules in random motion.

3.1 Pressure from Molecular Collisions

Pressure arises from countless molecular collisions with container walls. Consider one molecule of mass \(m\) moving with speed \(c_x\) in the x-direction:

\[\text{Change in momentum per collision} = 2mc_x\] \[\text{Time between collisions} = \frac{2L}{c_x}\] \[\text{Force from one molecule} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{mc_x^2}{L}\]

For \(N\) molecules with random motion, averaging over all directions (\(c_x^2 = c^2/3\)):

\[p = \frac{1}{3}\frac{Nm\langle c^2 \rangle}{V}\]

This is the fundamental equation of kinetic theory.

3.2 Connecting to Ideal Gas Law

Comparing \(pV = \frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle\) with \(pV = NkT\):

\[\frac{1}{3}Nm\langle c^2 \rangle = NkT\] \[\frac{1}{3}m\langle c^2 \rangle = kT\]

This leads to our next key result about molecular kinetic energy...

4. Molecular Kinetic Energy

The Key Result: From kinetic theory, we derive one of the most important relationships in thermal physics: the direct proportionality between temperature and molecular kinetic energy.

4.1 Derivation of the Key Equation

From the kinetic theory (Section 3.2), we have:

\[\frac{1}{3}m\langle c^2 \rangle = kT\]

Multiplying both sides by \(\frac{3}{2}\):

\[\frac{1}{2}m\langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT\]

The left side is exactly the average translational kinetic energy of a molecule:

\[\langle E_k \rangle = \frac{1}{2}m\langle c^2 \rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT\]
Average Translational Kinetic Energy

The average translational kinetic energy of a molecule in an ideal gas depends only on temperature:

\[\langle E_k \rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT = \frac{3}{2}\frac{RT}{N_A}\]

Key insight: At a given temperature, all ideal gas molecules have the same average kinetic energy, regardless of their mass!

4.2 Total Internal Energy

For \(N\) molecules, the total internal energy is:

\[U = N \times \frac{3}{2}kT = \frac{3}{2}NkT = \frac{3}{2}nRT\]

This shows that the internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on temperature, not on volume or pressure.

4.4 Degrees of Freedom

The factor \(\frac{3}{2}\) in the kinetic energy formula comes from the equipartition of energy:

  • Molecules have 3 independent directions of motion (x, y, z)
  • Each direction is called a degree of freedom
  • Each degree of freedom contributes \(\frac{1}{2}kT\) to the average energy
  • Total: \(3 \times \frac{1}{2}kT = \frac{3}{2}kT\)
Degrees of Freedom for Different Molecules:
  • Monatomic gas (e.g., He, Ne): 3 translational → \(U = \frac{3}{2}nRT\)
  • Diatomic gas (e.g., N₂, O₂): 3 translational + 2 rotational + 1 vibrational → \(U \approx \frac{5}{2}nRT\) (at moderate T)
Practical Example: At room temperature (300 K), nitrogen molecules (N₂) have average kinetic energy: \[\langle E_k \rangle = \frac{3}{2} \times 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 300 = 6.2 \times 10^{-21} \text{ J}\] This is tiny for one molecule, but enormous for a mole (about 3.7 kJ/mol).

5. Root-Mean-Square Speed

Why RMS? Molecules in a gas have a range of speeds. The average speed would be zero (equal numbers moving in opposite directions). We use root-mean-square to get a meaningful measure of typical molecular speed.
Definition: Root-Mean-Square Speed
\[c_{rms} = \sqrt{\langle c^2 \rangle} = \sqrt{\frac{3kT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}}\]

where \(M\) is the molar mass (kg mol⁻¹).

5.1 Key Relationships

From the equation \(c_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3kT}{m}}\):

  • \(c_{rms} \propto \sqrt{T}\) - higher temperature, faster molecules
  • \(c_{rms} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}}\) - lighter molecules move faster at same temperature
\[\frac{c_{rms1}}{c_{rms2}} = \sqrt{\frac{T_1}{T_2}} = \sqrt{\frac{m_2}{m_1}}\]
Example Calculation: At 300 K, nitrogen molecules (M = 0.028 kg/mol) have: \[c_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3 \times 8.31 \times 300}{0.028}} \approx 515 \text{ m/s}\] This is faster than the speed of sound in air!

6. Assumptions of Kinetic Theory

The kinetic theory model is based on several simplifying assumptions:

The Basic Assumptions
When do real gases deviate? Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at:
  • High pressure - molecular volume becomes significant
  • Low temperature - intermolecular forces become significant
