Introduction: What is Electromagnetic Induction?

Think about these experiences:
  • Why does a magnet attract iron but not copper?
  • How does an electric motor convert electrical energy to kinetic energy?
  • How does a generator produce electricity by rotating a coil in a magnetic field?
  • How do charged particles from the Sun create the aurora borealis?

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.0: Magnetic field patterns around magnets and current-carrying wires

All these phenomena share one fundamental principle: magnetic fields are created by moving electric charges. Let's discover the laws that govern them.

Key Insight: This unit contains some of the most important concepts in A-Level Physics. Lenz's Law and electromagnetic induction are frequently examined in Paper 2 and Paper 4.

1. Magnetic Field Basics

1.1 What is a Magnetic Field?

A magnetic field is a region of space in which a magnetic force is experienced by:

  • A current-carrying conductor
  • A moving charge
  • A permanent magnet
Key Properties:
  • Magnetic fields are invisible and intangible
  • Similar to electric fields - an objective existence of matter without our direct sense
  • Can be detected using a compass needle or iron filings

Direction of Magnetic Field: The direction at a point is defined as the direction of the N pole of a small magnetic needle when stationary at that point.

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.1.1: Bar Magnet and Magnetic Field Pattern detected by iron filings

1.2 Magnetic Field Lines

Magnetic field lines provide a visual representation of magnetic fields.

Rules for Magnetic Field Lines:
  1. Field lines emerge from N pole and enter S pole
  2. Field is strongest where lines are closest together
  3. Field lines never cross each other
  4. The direction of a field line shows the direction of force on a "free" N pole

1.4 Magnetic Flux Density (B)

Definition - Magnetic Flux Density (B):
The force per unit current per unit length acting on a conductor placed perpendicularly to the field.
Formula:
\[B = \frac{F}{Il}\]

Unit: Tesla (T) = N m-1 A-1
Earth's field: approximately 5 x 10-5 T

1.5 Ampere's Force

When a current-carrying wire is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force.

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.1.5: Fleming's Left-Hand Rule - Force on Current-Carrying Wire

First finger = Field (N to S), ThUmb = Motion/Force, SeCond finger = Current

Force Magnitude:
\[F = BIl \sin \theta\]

When wire is perpendicular to field (theta = 90 deg): F = BIl (maximum)

Why Does a Current-Carrying Wire Experience Force?

Deep Explanation - Field Interaction:
  1. When current flows, the wire produces its own magnetic field
  2. This field interacts with the external magnetic field
  3. On one side of the wire: fields add up (stronger)
  4. On the other side: fields cancel (weaker)
  5. The wire is pushed toward the weaker field side

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.1.6: Microscopic Explanation - Wire Force due to Field Superposition

1.6 Two Parallel Current-Carrying Wires

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.1.7: Two Parallel Wires - Same Direction Currents (Attraction)

Why Do Parallel Wires Attract or Repel?
  1. Same direction currents attract: Fields add up between wires, denser in middle = Attract
  2. Opposite direction currents repel: Fields cancel between wires, weaker in middle = Repel
Memory Tip:
Same direction - field "squeezed" in between - Attract
Opposite direction - field "canceled" in between - Repel

2. Lorentz Force - Force on Moving Charges

2.1 What is Lorentz Force?

The Lorentz force is the force exerted by a magnetic field on a moving charge.

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.2.1: Lorentz Force on a Moving Positive Charge

Magnitude:
\[F = Bqv \sin \theta\]
Direction: Left-Hand Rule (Modified)
  • First finger: Velocity (direction of positive charge movement)
  • ThUmb: Force
  • SeCond finger: Magnetic field
Important Distinction:
Left-Hand Rule TypeUsed For
Standard Fleming'sForce on current-carrying wire (Ampere's force)
Modified (for velocity)Force on moving charge (Lorentz force)

For negative charges (electrons): Direction is opposite to that for positive charges.

Key Property: Lorentz force does no work (always perpendicular to velocity). It only changes direction, not speed. The motion is circular.

2.2 Charged Particles in Uniform Magnetic Field

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.2.2: Circular Motion of Charged Particle in Uniform Magnetic Field

Why is the motion circular?
  1. Lorentz force F = Bqv is always perpendicular to velocity
  2. Perpendicular force only changes direction, not speed
  3. Constant speed + perpendicular force = uniform circular motion
Centripetal Force = Lorentz Force:
\[Bqv = \frac{mv^2}{r}\]
Radius of Path:
\[r = \frac{mv}{Bq} = \frac{p}{Bq}\]

r is proportional to momentum (mv) and inversely proportional to B and q

Time Period:
\[T = \frac{2\pi m}{Bq}\]

Independent of velocity! All particles of same q/m complete one circle in the same time

Key Insight: Since T is independent of velocity, faster particles have larger radius but complete the circle in the same time. This is the principle behind cyclotrons (particle accelerators).

2.3 Velocity Selector

A device that selects particles of a specific velocity using perpendicular electric and magnetic fields.

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.2.3: Velocity Selector - E and B fields are perpendicular to each other

How it works:
  • Electric force: FE = E q (acts upward)
  • Magnetic force: FB = B q v (acts downward for positive charge)
  • When balanced: E q = B q v
  • Selected velocity: v = E / B
Balanced (Selected) Condition:
\[v = \frac{E}{B}\]

Only particles with v = E/B pass through undeflected

2.4 The Hall Effect

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, a voltage develops perpendicular to both current and field - this is the Hall effect.

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.2.4: Hall Effect - Voltage develops across width of conductor

Hall Voltage:
\[V_H = \frac{IB}{nqd}\]

Where n = carrier density, d = thickness of conductor

Applications of Hall Effect:
  • Measuring magnetic field strength B
  • Determining the sign of charge carriers (positive or negative)
  • Gaussmeters (magnetic field measuring devices)

3. Electromagnetic Induction

3.1 Magnetic Flux and Flux Linkage

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.1: Magnetic Flux - BA cos theta

Magnetic Flux:
\[\Phi = BA \cos \theta\]

Unit: Weber (Wb = T m2)

Magnetic Flux Linkage:
\[N\Phi = NBA \cos \theta\]
Key Difference:
  • Flux Phi is independent of coil turns N
  • Flux linkage NPhi depends on number of turns N

3.2 Lenz's Law - Deep Dive

Lenz's Law:
"The direction of an induced current (or e.m.f.) is such that it opposes the change producing it."
This is one of the most frequently examined laws in A-Level Physics!

3.3 Three Questions for Lenz's Law

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.2: Lenz's Law Three-Question Analysis

QuestionAnswer
1. Who is opposing?The induced current's magnetic field
2. What is being opposed?The change in magnetic flux (increase or decrease)
3. How does it oppose?By creating a field in the opposite direction

3.4 Opposition is NOT Prevention

This is the MOST COMMON MISTAKE in exams!

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.3: Opposition is NOT Prevention - flux still changes, just more slowly

SituationWhat HappensResult
Flux increasingInduced field opposes increaseIncrease is slower (but still increases!)
Flux decreasingInduced field opposes decreaseDecrease is slower (but still decreases!)
Analogy: Like pushing two N poles together - you feel repulsion, but the magnets still move closer if you push hard enough.

3.5 Lenz's Law = Energy Conservation

Why does Lenz's Law exist? It is a direct consequence of energy conservation!

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.4: Lenz's Law and Energy Conservation

Wrong assumption (would violate energy conservation):

If induced current attracted the approaching magnet:
Magnet accelerates + Energy generated = Energy created from nothing!
Correct explanation:
When magnet approaches, induced current repels it:
External work must be done to overcome repulsion
This work becomes electrical energy
Energy conserved!
Key Insight: Lenz's Law is NOT an arbitrary rule - it is a direct consequence of energy conservation.

3.6 Lenz's Law - Four Cases

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.5: Four Cases of Magnet Approaching/Leaving Coil

CaseMagnet MotionFlux ChangeInduced FieldPolarity at Face
1N pole approachingIncreasingOpposes (repels)N pole

Memory Tip:
"N approaching: N at face (repulsion)"
"N leaving: S at face (attraction)"

3.7 Faraday's Law

Definition:
The magnitude of induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage.

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.6: Faraday's Law - EMF from changing flux

\[\mathcal{E} = -N\frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}\]

Negative sign reflects Lenz's Law (opposition)

EMF from Moving Wire:
\[\mathcal{E} = Blv\]

For wire moving perpendicular to field

3.8 Bicycle Dynamo Analysis

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.7: Bicycle Dynamo - Mechanical to Electrical Energy Conversion

Why is it harder to pedal when the light is ON?
  1. Turning generator causes coil to cut magnetic field
  2. Induced e.m.f. generated
  3. With circuit closed (light ON): induced current flows
  4. According to Lenz's Law, induced current's magnetic field opposes the rotation
  5. External work must be done to overcome this opposition
  6. Mechanical energy converts to Electrical energy
When light is OFF: Circuit is open, no current flows, no opposition, easier to pedal!

3.9 Applications

1. AC Generator (Alternator)

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.8: AC Generator - Coil rotating in magnetic field produces alternating EMF

How it works:
  • Coil rotates in magnetic field
  • Angle between coil normal and field changes continuously
  • Flux Phi = NBA cos(omega t) varies sinusoidally
  • EMF induced: epsilon = epsilon_0 sin(omega t)
Output EMF:
\[ arepsilon = \varepsilon_0 \sin(\omega t)\]

2. Transformers

[IMAGE PLACEHOLDER]

Fig 20.3.9: Transformer - Step up or step down voltage using electromagnetic induction

Transformer Equation:
\[ rac{V_p}{V_s} = rac{N_p}{N_s}\]

AC only - DC doesn't work (flux must change)

Important: Transformers only work with AC because the flux must be changing to induce EMF in the secondary coil.
