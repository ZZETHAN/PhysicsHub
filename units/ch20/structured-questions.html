Q1 Ampère's Force
[2 marks]

Source: Ex_20.1.2 Ampere's Force 1.pdf

A stiff straight copper wire XY is held fixed in a uniform magnetic field of flux density $2.6 \times 10^{-3}$ T, as shown in Fig.

The wire XY has length 4.7 cm and makes an angle of 34° with the magnetic field.

Calculate the force on the wire due to a constant current of 5.4 A in the wire. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

Solution:

M1Use formula: $F = BIl \sin \theta$
M1$F = 2.6 \times 10^{-3} \times 5.4 \times 0.047 \times \sin(34°)$
C1$F = 3.6 \times 10^{-4}$ N (accept $3.61 \times 10^{-4}$ N)
Q2 Ampère's Force
[10 marks]

Source: Ex_20.1.2 Ampere's Force 1.pdf

(a) Define the tesla. [2]

(b) Two long straight vertical wires X and Y are separated by a distance, as shown in Fig.

(i) Calculate the current in wire X. [2]

(ii) The magnetic flux density B at a distance x from a long straight current-carrying wire is given by the expression $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi x}$, where $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ H m⁻¹. Calculate the magnetic flux density at wire Y due to the current in wire X. [2]

(iii) A current of 9.3 A is now switched on in wire Y. Use your answer in (ii) to calculate the force per unit length on wire Y. [2]

(c) The currents in the two wires in (b)(iii) are not equal. Explain whether the force per unit length on the two wires will be the same, or different. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [2 marks]

C1Tesla is defined as the magnetic flux density that produces a force of 1 N on a conductor of length 1 m carrying a current of 1 A at right angles to the field
C1Unit: Tesla (T)

(b)(i) [2 marks]

M1Use $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$ with $r = 0.05$ m
C1$I_X = 2.7$ A (or shown calculation)

(b)(ii) [2 marks]

M1$B = \frac{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 2.7}{2\pi \times 0.05}$
C1$B = 1.08 \times 10^{-5}$ T

(b)(iii) [2 marks]

M1$F/L = B \times I_Y = 1.08 \times 10^{-5} \times 9.3$
C1$F/L = 1.0 \times 10^{-4}$ N m⁻¹

(c) [2 marks]

B1Force per unit length on each wire is given by $F/L = \frac{\mu_0 I_1 I_2}{2\pi d}$
B1Since the formula is symmetric, the force per unit length on each wire is equal in magnitude (Newton's Third Law)
Q3 Ampère's Force
[3 marks]

Source: Ex_20.1.3 Ampere's Force 2.pdf

Two long straight parallel wires P and Q carry currents into the plane of the paper, as shown in Fig.

The current in P is I and the current in Q is 2I.

(a) On Fig., draw an arrow to show the direction of the magnetic field at wire Q due to the current in wire P. Label this arrow B. [1]

(b) On Fig., draw another arrow to show the direction of the force acting on wire Q due to the current in wire P. Label this arrow F. [1]

(c) State, with a reason, how the magnitude of the force acting on wire P compares with the magnitude of the force acting on wire Q. [1]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

C1Arrow showing magnetic field direction at Q (clockwise around P using right-hand grip rule, or correct tangent direction)

(b) [1 mark]

C1Arrow showing force direction on Q (attraction towards wire P, using Fleming's left-hand rule or understanding that parallel currents attract)

Q4 Lorentz Force
[4 marks]

Source: Ex_20.2.1 Lorentz Force 1.pdf

An electron is moving at $1.0 \times 10^6$ m s⁻¹ in a uniform magnetic field of flux density 0.50 T, as shown in Fig.

Calculate the force on the electron when it is moving:

(a) at right angles to the field [2]

(b) at an angle of 45° to the field. [2]

e = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [2 marks]

M1Use $F = Bqv \sin \theta$ with $\theta = 90°$

Q5 Lorentz Force
[3 marks]

Source: Ex_20.2.1 Lorentz Force 1.pdf

A beam of electrons is produced by an 'electron gun', and magnets are used to apply a magnetic field as shown in Fig.

A beam of electrons is deflected as it crosses a magnetic field.

Explain why the electron beam is deflected in a curved path rather than a straight line. [3]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

Solution: [3 marks]

B1The magnetic force $F = Bqv \sin \theta$ acts perpendicular to the velocity of the electrons
B1A perpendicular force causes circular motion (similar to tension providing centripetal force)
B1The beam follows a curved path because the force is always perpendicular to the instantaneous direction of motion, continuously changing the direction but not the speed
Q6 Lorentz Force
[6 marks]

Source: Ex_20.2.1 Lorentz Force 1.pdf

An electron is travelling at right angles to a uniform magnetic field of flux density 1.2 mT, as shown in Fig.

The speed of the electron is $8.0 \times 10^6$ m s⁻¹. Calculate the radius of the circle described by this electron. [6]

e = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C and mₑ = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

Solution: [6 marks]

M1Magnetic force provides centripetal force: $Bqv = \frac{mv^2}{r}$
M1Rearrange: $r = \frac{mv}{Bq}$
M1Substitute values: $r = \frac{9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 8.0 \times 10^6}{1.2 \times 10^{-3} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19}}$
C1$r = \frac{7.288 \times 10^{-24}}{1.92 \times 10^{-22}}$
C1$r = 3.8 \times 10^{-2}$ m = 3.8 cm (accept 3.7-4.0 cm)
Q7 Lorentz Force
[5 marks]

Source: Ex_20.2.2 Lorentz Force 2.pdf

A velocity selector is used to select ions of a specific speed, as shown in Fig.

(a) State the directions of the magnetic and electric forces on a positively charged ion travelling towards the slit S. [1]

(b) Calculate the speed of an ion emerging from the slit S when the magnetic flux density is 0.30 T and the electric field strength is $1.5 \times 10^3$ V m⁻¹. [2]

(c) Explain why ions travelling with a speed greater than your answer to (b) will not emerge from the slit. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [1 mark]

C1Both forces act in opposite directions (electric force upward, magnetic force downward for positive ion with velocity toward slit)

(b) [2 marks]

M1For undeflected ions: $qvB = qE$, so $v = E/B$
C1$v = \frac{1.5 \times 10^3}{0.30} = 5.0 \times 10^3$ m s⁻¹

(c) [2 marks]

B1For ions with $v > E/B$: magnetic force $Bqv$ > electric force $qE$
B1So net force is downward (magnetic dominates), ion deflected away from slit
Q8 Lorentz Force
[6 marks]

Source: Ex_20.2.2 Lorentz Force 2.pdf

A Hall probe is designed to operate with a steady current of 0.020 A in a semiconductor slice of thickness 0.05 mm, as shown in Fig.

The number density of charge carriers (electrons) in the semiconductor is $1.5 \times 10^{23}$ m⁻³.

(astrong> (a) Calculate the Hall voltage that will result when the probe is placed at right angles to a magnetic field of flux density 0.10 T. [4]

(b) Explain why the current in the Hall probe must be maintained at a constant value. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [4 marks]

M1Use Hall voltage formula: $V = \frac{BI}{nqt}$ where t = thickness
M1Current density: $J = nqv$, so $I = nqvAt$ where A is cross-sectional area
M1$V = \frac{BIA}{nqt} = \frac{0.10 \times 0.020 \times 1}{1.5 \times 10^{23} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \times 0.05 \times 10^{-3}}$
C1$V = 1.7 \times 10^{-3}$ V = 1.7 mV

(b) [2 marks]

B1Hall voltage is proportional to current ($V \propto I$) at constant B
B1If current varies, Hall voltage varies, making calibration inaccurate
Q9 EMI
[4 marks]

Source: Ex_20.3.1 Electromagnetic induction.pdf

When an aircraft flies from east to west, its wings are an electrical conductor cutting across the Earth's magnetic flux. In the northern hemisphere, state which wingtip (left or right) will become positive. State and explain what will happen to this wingtip in the southern hemisphere. [4]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

Solution: [4 marks]

B1In northern hemisphere, Earth's magnetic field has a downward component (pointing into Earth)
B1Using Fleming's right-hand rule (generator rule): motion west, field down, current flows from left to right wingtip
B1Right wingtip becomes positive (conventional current flows from left to right)
B1In southern hemisphere, Earth's field points upward, so left wingtip becomes positive
Q10 EMI
[5 marks]

Source: Ex_20.3.2 Lenz's law.pdf

A small solenoid of area of cross section $1.6 \times 10^{-3}$ m² is placed inside a larger solenoid of area of cross-section $6.4 \times 10^{-3}$ m², as shown in Fig.

The larger solenoid has 600 turns and is attached to a d.c. power supply to create a magnetic field. The smaller solenoid has 3000 turns.

(a) Compare the magnetic flux in the two solenoids. [1]

(b) Compare the magnetic flux linkage in the two solenoids. [1]

(c) The current in the large solenoid is reduced uniformly to zero in a time of 0.50 s. Calculate the average e.m.f. induced in the small solenoid. [3]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [1 mark]

C1Flux is the same in both solenoids (flux through small solenoid equals flux through large solenoid since same B field and same core)

(b) [1 mark]

C1Flux linkage = N × Φ. Large solenoid: 600 × Φ. Small solenoid: 3000 × Φ. Small solenoid has 5× greater flux linkage (3000:600 = 5:1)

(c) [3 marks]

M1Use Faraday's law: $\varepsilon = -N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$
M1Small solenoid: $\varepsilon = 3000 \times \frac{\Phi}{0.50}$ where $\Phi = B \times A_{small}$
C1Without values given, answer should be in terms of initial flux: $\varepsilon = 6000 \Phi$ V or calculated value based on given B

Q12 EMI
[6 marks]

Source: Ex_20.3.3 Faraday's law and applications.pdf

A transformer is used to change 240 V alternating voltage to 6.0 V, as shown in Fig.

(a) The primary coil has 2000 turns. Calculate the number of turns on the secondary coil. [2]

(b) The current in the secondary coil is 2.0 A. Assuming an ideal transformer, calculate the current in the primary coil. [2]

(c) State one way in which a real transformer differs from an ideal transformer. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [2 marks]

M1Use transformer equation: $\frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$
C1$\frac{6.0}{240} = \frac{N_s}{2000}$, so $N_s = \frac{6.0 \times 2000}{240} = 50$ turns

(b) [2 marks]

M1For ideal transformer: $V_p I_p = V_s I_s$ (power conserved)
C1$I_p = \frac{V_s I_s}{V_p} = \frac{6.0 \times 2.0}{240} = 0.050$ A = 50 mA

(c) [2 marks]

B1Real transformers have energy losses: resistance in windings (copper losses), eddy currents in core, hysteresis losses
B1Real transformers are not 100% efficient (typically 95-99%)
Q13 EMI
[6 marks]

Source: Ex_20.3.4 Revision.pdf

A rectangular coil ABCD is rotated in a uniform magnetic field, as shown in Fig.

The coil has 80 turns and each turn has an area of $4.0 \times 10^{-3}$ m². The coil is rotated at 4.0 revolutions per second in a uniform magnetic field of flux density 0.25 T.

(a) Calculate the maximum e.m.f. induced in the coil. [3]

(b) State the position of the coil when the e.m.f. has its maximum value. [2]

(c) The coil is now rotated at 8.0 revolutions per second. State the new maximum e.m.f. [1]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [3 marks]

M1Angular velocity: $\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi \times 4.0 = 8\pi$ rad s⁻¹
M1Maximum emf: $\varepsilon_{max} = NAB\omega = 80 \times 0.25 \times 4.0 \times 10^{-3} \times 8\pi$
C1$\varepsilon_{max} = 2.5$ V (accept 2.5-2.6 V)

(b) [2 marks]

B1E.m.f. is maximum when coil is parallel to magnetic field lines (i.e., when plane of coil is perpendicular to B)
B1This is when the coil sides are cutting through maximum flux (rate of change of flux is maximum)

(c) [1 mark]

C1Since $\varepsilon_{max} \propto \omega \propto f$, doubling frequency doubles emf: new $\varepsilon_{max} = 5.0$ V
Q14 EMI
[6 marks]

Source: Ex_20.3.4 Revision.pdf

A coil of insulated wire is wound on to a soft-iron core. The magnetic flux density B in the core is varied with current I, as shown in Fig.

Use the graph to determine:

(a) The magnetic flux linkage of the coil when I = 2.0 A. [2]

(b) The e.m.f. induced in the coil when the current decreases uniformly from 2.0 A to 0 in a time of 0.10 s. [4]

Mark Scheme
Mark Distribution: C = correct answer only, A = any order, B = independent, M = method

(a) [2 marks]

M1Read B value from graph at I = 2.0 A (approximately 1.2 T)
M1Flux linkage = N × B × A. Assuming N = 1000 turns and A = area from question
C1Flux linkage = NBA = (number of turns) × B × cross-sectional area

(b) [4 marks]

M1Use Faraday's law: $\varepsilon = -N \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} = -N \frac{\Delta(B A)}{\Delta t}$
M1Rate of change of flux = $\frac{\Delta B}{\Delta t} \times A$
M1Since B decreases uniformly from 1.2 T to 0 in 0.10 s: $\frac{\Delta B}{\Delta t} = \frac{1.2}{0.10} = 12$ T s⁻¹
C1$\varepsilon = N \times A \times 12$ V (value depends on coil area)
