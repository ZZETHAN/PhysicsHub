Introduction: What is Temperature?

Think about these experiences:
  • Why does a metal spoon feel colder than a wooden spoon at room temperature?
  • How do thermometers actually measure temperature?
  • Why does ice take so long to melt even when the surrounding air is warm?
  • Why does water in a swimming pool stay cooler than the surrounding air on a hot day?

Temperature is one of the most familiar concepts in physics, yet its precise definition requires careful understanding of thermal equilibrium and energy transfer.

Connection to Previous Units: In Unit 5 (Work, Energy and Power), we learned about energy transfer. In this unit, we explore how energy transfers as heat and how temperature changes relate to energy changes.
Paper 4 Examination Focus: Expect calculations involving specific heat capacity and specific latent heat. Understand the difference between temperature and internal energy. Be able to explain thermal equilibrium using the zeroth law.

1. The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

The Fundamental Question: How can we define temperature in a way that is independent of any particular thermometer?

Before we can measure temperature, we need to understand when two objects have the same temperature. This leads us to the concept of thermal equilibrium.

Definition: Thermal Equilibrium

Two systems are in thermal equilibrium if there is no net flow of thermal energy between them when they are brought into thermal contact.

Now consider three systems: A, B, and C. The 1. Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics states:

1. Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If two thermodynamic systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

Mathematically: If A ↔ C and B ↔ C, then A ↔ B

[插图占位符 - 热力学第零定律示意图：三个物体A、B、C的热平衡关系]

1.1 Why is this Law Important?

The Zeroth Law allows us to:

  • Define temperature: Two objects have the same temperature if and only if they are in thermal equilibrium
  • Calibrate thermometers: A thermometer (system C) can be used to compare temperatures of systems A and B without direct contact between A and B
  • Establish temperature scales: We can create reproducible temperature measurements
Common Misconception: Being "cold" or "hot" is subjective. Temperature is an objective physical quantity defined by thermal equilibrium. Two objects at the same temperature don't exchange heat, regardless of their sizes or materials.

1.2 Practical Example: The Thermometer Principle

When you use a thermometer to measure your body temperature:

  1. The thermometer comes into thermal contact with your body
  2. Heat flows until thermal equilibrium is reached
  3. At equilibrium, the thermometer reading equals your body temperature
  4. The Zeroth Law guarantees that any other object in equilibrium with your body would give the same reading

2. Temperature Scales

The Challenge: How do we create a numerical scale for temperature? We need fixed points that are reproducible and universal.

To establish a temperature scale, we need:

  • A thermometric property (a physical property that changes with temperature)
  • Two fixed points (reference temperatures)
  • An agreed rule for interpolation between the fixed points

2.1 The Celsius Scale

Originally defined using the properties of water:

Fixed Point Temperature Definition
Ice point (lower fixed point) 0°C Temperature of pure melting ice at standard atmospheric pressure
Steam point (upper fixed point) 100°C Temperature of steam from boiling water at standard atmospheric pressure
Important Note: The original Celsius definition had problems:
  • The boiling point of water varies with pressure
  • Different thermometers give slightly different readings between 0°C and 100°C

2.2 The Absolute (Kelvin) Scale

The Kelvin scale is based on thermodynamic principles and is independent of any particular substance:

The Kelvin Scale
  • Zero point: Absolute zero (0 K) - the lowest possible temperature where particles have minimum kinetic energy
  • Unit size: Same as Celsius (1 K = 1°C)
  • Triple point of water: Exactly 273.16 K (where ice, water, and steam coexist in equilibrium)
Conversion between scales: \[T(\text{K}) = T(°\text{C}) + 273.15\] \[T(°\text{C}) = T(\text{K}) - 273.15\]

[插图占位符 - 温标对比图：Celsius、Kelvin、Fahrenheit 对比]

2.3 Why Use Kelvin in Physics?

  • No negative values: Important for gas laws and thermodynamic calculations
  • Absolute zero is physically meaningful: It represents zero molecular motion
  • Ratios are meaningful: 200 K is twice as hot as 100 K (in terms of average kinetic energy)
Exam Tip: Always use Kelvin for gas law calculations (PV = nRT) and calculations involving absolute temperature. Use Celsius for temperature changes (ΔT) since 1°C = 1 K.

3. Thermometric Properties

The Key Question: What physical properties can we use to measure temperature? And why do different thermometers sometimes give different readings?
Definition: Thermometric Property

A physical property that changes continuously and monotonically with temperature, used to measure temperature.

3.1 Common Thermometric Properties

Liquid-in-glass

Property: Volume expansion of mercury or alcohol

Range: Limited by freezing/boiling points of liquid

Thermocouple

Property: Electromotive force (emf) generated at junction of two dissimilar metals

Range: Very wide, fast response

Resistance Thermometer

Property: Electrical resistance of a metal (usually platinum)

Range: Very accurate, wide range

Gas Thermometer

Property: Pressure of a gas at constant volume (or volume at constant pressure)

Range: Most accurate, used to define Kelvin scale

3.2 The Problem: Different Thermometers, Different Scales

If we calibrate two different thermometers at 0°C and 100°C, they will agree at those points. But they may give different readings at intermediate temperatures!

[插图占位符 - 不同温度计读数差异示意图]

This happens because thermometric properties don't all vary linearly with temperature in the same way. For example:

  • The expansion of mercury is not perfectly linear with thermodynamic temperature
  • The resistance of platinum changes differently than the emf of a thermocouple
Key Point: Only the constant-volume gas thermometer gives readings that correspond to thermodynamic temperature (Kelvin). All other thermometers are calibrated against it.

4. Specific Heat Capacity

The Question: Why does a swimming pool take longer to warm up in the sun than a bucket of water? Why does sand get hotter than water at the beach?

Different substances require different amounts of energy to raise their temperature. This property is called specific heat capacity.

Definition: Specific Heat Capacity (c)

The amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

\[c = \frac{E}{m\Delta T}\]

Unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ (or J kg⁻¹ °C⁻¹)

Energy Transfer Equation: \[E = mc\Delta T\]

where E = energy transferred, m = mass, c = specific heat capacity, ΔT = temperature change

4.1 Values of Specific Heat Capacity

Substance Specific Heat Capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) Notes
Water 4200 Very high - explains why water is used in heating/cooling systems
Ice 2100 About half that of liquid water
Aluminium 900 Good heat conductor, used in cookware
Iron/Steel 450 Lower than aluminium
Copper 385 Good conductor, heats up quickly
Lead 130 Very low - easy to heat up
Water's High Specific Heat Capacity: Water has one of the highest specific heat capacities of common substances. This explains:
  • Why oceans moderate coastal climates (slow to heat up, slow to cool down)
  • Why water is used in car radiators and central heating systems
  • Why deserts have large day-night temperature variations (low moisture)

4.2 Experimental Determination

Method of Mixtures: A heated solid is placed in cool water in an insulated container. By conservation of energy:

\[\text{Heat lost by solid} = \text{Heat gained by water} + \text{Heat gained by container}\] \[m_s c_s (T_s - T_f) = m_w c_w (T_f - T_w) + m_c c_c (T_f - T_w)\]

Electrical Method: An electric heater is used to heat a known mass of substance:

\[E = Pt = IVt = mc\Delta T\] \[c = \frac{IVt}{m\Delta T}\]
Common Error: When mixing hot and cold water, remember that the final temperature is the same for both. Use ΔT = T_final - T_initial for each substance, paying attention to signs.

5. Specific Latent Heat

The Question: When you boil water, the temperature stays at 100°C even though you're adding heat. Where does this energy go?

During phase changes (melting, boiling), energy is used to break intermolecular bonds rather than increase kinetic energy (temperature). This energy is called latent heat.

Definition: Specific Latent Heat of Fusion (Lₜ)

The energy required to change 1 kg of substance from solid to liquid (or vice versa) at constant temperature.

\[E = mL_f\]

Unit: J kg⁻¹ | For water: Lₜ = 3.34 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹

Definition: Specific Latent Heat of Vaporization (Lᵥ)

The energy required to change 1 kg of substance from liquid to gas (or vice versa) at constant temperature.

\[E = mL_v\]

Unit: J kg⁻¹ | For water: Lᵥ = 2.26 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹

[插图占位符 - 相变能量图：加热曲线，显示温度变化和潜热阶段]

5.1 Why is Lᵥ > Lₜ for Water?

Vaporization requires about 7 times more energy than fusion because:

  • In melting: Only some intermolecular bonds are broken; molecules remain close together
  • In vaporization: All intermolecular bonds must be broken; molecules separate completely
  • Work against atmosphere: Vaporization also requires energy to expand against atmospheric pressure
Practical Applications:
  • Steam burns are worse than boiling water burns: Steam releases extra latent heat when it condenses on skin
  • Refrigeration: Uses latent heat of vaporization to absorb heat from inside the fridge
  • Cooling by evaporation: Sweating cools your body as water evaporates

5.2 Complete Heating Curve Analysis

When heating ice at -20°C to steam at 120°C, the process involves five stages:

  1. Ice warming (-20°C to 0°C): \(E = mc_i\Delta T\) where \(c_i = 2100 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}\)
  2. Melting (0°C): \(E = mL_f\) (no temperature change)
  3. Water warming (0°C to 100°C): \(E = mc_w\Delta T\) where \(c_w = 4200 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}\)
  4. Boiling (100°C): \(E = mL_v\) (no temperature change)
  5. Steam warming (100°C to 120°C): \(E = mc_s\Delta T\) where \(c_s \approx 2000 \text{ J kg}^{-1} \text{ K}^{-1}\)
Exam Tip: In calculations involving phase changes, always identify which stage(s) the process involves. Calculate each stage separately and sum the energies.
