P4 Style Specific Heat Capacity
[8 marks]

(a) Define specific heat capacity and state its unit. [2]

(b) A student wishes to determine the specific heat capacity of aluminium using an electrical method. A 0.50 kg block of aluminium is heated using an electric heater rated at 12 V, 4.0 A. The initial temperature of the block is 22°C. After heating for 5.0 minutes, the temperature rises to 42°C. [6]

(i) Calculate the electrical energy supplied to the heater. [2]

(ii) Calculate the specific heat capacity of aluminium. [2]

(iii) Suggest why the value obtained is likely to be higher than the accepted value, and explain one modification to reduce this error. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork/Definition | M = Method | A = Answer
(a) - Definition [2 marks]
B1
Energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K
B1
Unit: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
(b)(i) - Energy Calculation [2 marks]
M1
Energy = IVt = 4.0 × 12 × (5.0 × 60)
A1
14,400 J (accept 14.4 kJ)
(ii) - Specific Heat Capacity [2 marks]
M1
c = E/(mΔT) = 14400/(0.50 × 20)
A1
1440 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ (ecf from energy value)
(iii) - Error Analysis [2 marks]
E1
Heat loss to surroundings / not all heat transferred to block
E1
Insulate the block / use lid / polish surface / reduce heating time
Examiner Tip: Remember to convert time to seconds. 5.0 minutes = 300 s, not 5.0 s!
P4 Style Latent Heat
[6 marks]

(a) Explain why the temperature of a substance remains constant during a phase change, even though thermal energy is being supplied. [2]

(b) An electric kettle is used to boil water. The kettle contains 0.80 kg of water at an initial temperature of 20°C. The kettle is rated at 2.0 kW. [4]

Given: specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, specific latent heat of vaporization of water = 2.3 × 10⁶ J kg⁻¹

(i) Calculate the time taken for the water to reach its boiling point. [2]

(ii) Calculate the additional time required to boil away 0.10 kg of water. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork/Definition | M = Method | A = Answer
(a) - Explanation [2 marks]
B1
Energy is used to break intermolecular bonds / increase potential energy
B1
No increase in kinetic energy of molecules / no temperature change
(b)(i) - Heating Time [2 marks]
M1
Energy = mcΔT = 0.80 × 4200 × 80
A1
134 s (accept 135 s or 2.2 min)
(ii) - Vaporization Time [2 marks]
M1
Energy = mL = 0.10 × 2.3 × 10⁶
A1
115 s (accept 2.3 × 10⁵ ÷ 2000)
Examiner Tip: Note that boiling away water takes almost as long as heating it from 20°C to 100°C! This shows how large the latent heat of vaporization is compared to specific heat capacity.
P4 Style Method of Mixtures
[7 marks]

A piece of metal of mass 0.15 kg is heated to 100°C in boiling water. It is then quickly transferred to a copper calorimeter of mass 0.20 kg containing 0.40 kg of water at 25°C. The final temperature of the mixture is 28.5°C. [6]

Given: specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹, specific heat capacity of copper = 390 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹

(a) Explain why the metal must be transferred quickly to the calorimeter. [1]

(b) Calculate the specific heat capacity of the metal. [4]

(c) Suggest one source of error in this experiment and its effect on the calculated value. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: C = Comprehension | M = Method | A = Answer | E = Evaluation
(a) - Explanation [1 mark]
C1
To reduce heat loss to surroundings / keep initial temperature at 100°C
(b) - Calculation [4 marks]
M1
Heat lost by metal = heat gained by water + heat gained by calorimeter
M1
0.15 × c × 71.5 = 0.40 × 4200 × 3.5 + 0.20 × 390 × 3.5
M1
Right side = 5880 + 273 = 6153 J
A1
c = 570 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ (accept 574 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)
(c) - Error Analysis [2 marks]
E1
Heat loss to surroundings / metal not at 100°C / water splashed out
E1
Calculated c would be too high (or consistent with error stated)
P4 Style Temperature Scales
[5 marks]

(a) State the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics and explain how it enables temperature measurement. [3]

(b) A thermometer shows a reading of -40°C. Calculate this temperature in kelvin. [1]

(c) Explain why the Kelvin scale is preferred over the Celsius scale in thermodynamic calculations. [1]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork/Definition | M = Method | A = Answer
(a) - Zeroth Law [3 marks]
B1
If A and C are in thermal equilibrium, and B and C are in thermal equilibrium, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium
B1
Allows a thermometer (C) to be used to compare temperatures of A and B
B1
Two objects in thermal equilibrium have the same temperature
(b) - Temperature Conversion [1 mark]
A1
233 K (accept 273 + (-40) = 233)
(c) - Kelvin Scale Advantage [1 mark]
B1
Kelvin has absolute zero / no negative values / ratios are meaningful / directly proportional to average kinetic energy
💡 Exam Tips:
  • Always show your working clearly with the formula first
  • Remember to convert temperatures to kelvin for gas law calculations only
  • For method of mixtures: Heat lost = Heat gained (conservation of energy)
  • During phase changes, temperature remains constant while energy is absorbed/released
