CIE 9702/42/M/J/2022 Orbital Motion
[6 marks]

A satellite of mass \(m\) orbits a planet of mass \(M\) in a circular orbit of radius \(r\).

(a) Show that the orbital speed \(v\) of the satellite is given by: [4]

\[v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}\]

(b) The satellite moves to a new orbit with a different radius. Explain why the speed of the satellite changes. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
B1
Equates gravitational force to centripetal force: \(F = \frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}\)
B1
Cancels \(m\) and one \(r\) to give: \(\frac{GM}{r} = v^2\)
B1
Rearranges to show: \(v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}\)
M1
Explanation: Gravitational force changes (decreases as \(r\) increases / increases as \(r\) decreases) [1]
B1
This requires a different speed for circular orbit (or gravitational force provides different centripetal force)
Examiner Tip: Common error - forgetting that gravitational force depends on \(1/r^2\) while centripetal force depends on \(1/r\). Always start with \(F_g = F_c\).
CIE 9702/42/O/N/2021 Escape Velocity
[4 marks]

(a) Define gravitational potential at a point. [1]

(b) By considering energy, derive an expression for the escape velocity \(v_0\) from the surface of a planet of mass \(M\) and radius \(R\). [3]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
B1
Work done per unit mass bringing (small test) mass from infinity to the point [Accept: work done per unit mass / energy per unit mass]
B1
Kinetic energy at surface = \(0 - (-\frac{GMm}{R})\) or \(\frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = \frac{GMm}{R}\)
B1
Cancels \(m\): \(\frac{1}{2}v_0^2 = \frac{GM}{R}\)
B1
Rearranges to: \(v_0 = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}\) [Accept \(\sqrt{2gR}\)]
Examiner Tip: Remember the negative sign in gravitational potential! At surface: \(\phi = -\frac{GM}{R}\). At infinity: \(\phi = 0\). The change is \(\frac{GM}{R}\).
CIE 9702/42/M/J/2023 Gravitational Field Strength
[3 marks]

The gravitational field strength \(g\) at the surface of a planet is \(8.6\,\text{N kg}^{-1}\). The radius of the planet is \(3.4 \times 10^6\,\text{m}\).

(a) Calculate the mass of the planet. [3]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
B1
Uses: \(g = \frac{GM}{R^2}\) or \((3.4 \times 10^6)^2 \times 8.6 = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times M\)
B1
Rearrangement: \(M = \frac{gR^2}{G}\)
B1
Answer: \(M = 1.5 \times 10^{24}\,\text{kg}\) [Accept 1.48-1.52 \times 10^{24}]
Examiner Tip: Always check your powers of 10! Common error: forgetting to square the radius. Also remember that \(R\) is the distance from the center, not the height above surface.

A satellite of mass \(m\) orbits the Earth in a circular orbit of radius \(r\), as shown in the diagram. The Earth has mass \(M\) and radius \(R\).

(a) Show that the kinetic energy \(E_k\) of the satellite is given by: [2]

\[E_k = \frac{GMm}{2r}\]

(b) The total energy \(E_T\) of the satellite is the sum of its kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy. Show that: [2]

\[E_T = -\frac{GMm}{2r}\]

(c) Explain why the total energy is negative. [1]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
M1
Part (a): Equates \(\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}\) [1], uses \(E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\) to derive \(E_k = \frac{GMm}{2r}\) [1]
M1
Part (b): States \(E_T = E_k + \phi\) or \(\frac{GMm}{2r} + (-\frac{GMm}{r})\) [1], simplifies to \(-\frac{GMm}{2r}\) [1]
B1
Part (c): Bound system / work must be done to separate masses / satellite cannot escape to infinity with current energy [any valid point]
Examiner Tip: The negative total energy indicates a bound orbit. Notice that \(E_k = -E_T\) and \(E_k = \frac{1}{2}|E_p|\) - this is the virial theorem for circular orbits.
CIE 9702/42/M/J/2021 Kepler's Third Law
[6 marks]

A geostationary satellite orbits the Earth with a period of 24 hours, remaining above the same point on the Earth's equator.

Data for Earth:

  • Mass \(M_E = 6.0 \times 10^{24}\,\text{kg}\)
  • Radius \(R_E = 6.4 \times 10^6\,\text{m}\)

(a) Show that the radius \(r\) of the geostationary orbit is given by: [2]

\[r^3 = \frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}\]

(b) Calculate the radius \(r\) of the geostationary orbit. [2]

(c) Calculate the speed of the geostationary satellite. [2]

Mark Scheme
Mark Types: B = Bookwork | M = Method | A = Answer
M1
Part (a): Equates gravitational and centripetal forces [1], substitutes \(v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}\) and rearranges to give \(r^3 = \frac{GMT^2}{4\pi^2}\) [1]
M1
Part (b): Correct substitution: \(r^3 = \frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 6.0 \times 10^{24} \times (24 \times 3600)^2}{4\pi^2}\) [1], answer: \(r \approx 4.2 \times 10^7\,\text{m}\) (about 6.6 Earth radii) [1]
M1
Part (c): Uses \(v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}\) or \(v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}\) [1], answer: \(v \approx 3.1\,\text{km s}^{-1}\) [1]
Examiner Tip: Remember to convert 24 hours to seconds (86400 s)! Geostationary satellites orbit at about 36000 km above Earth's surface, much higher than low-Earth orbit satellites (400-800 km).
