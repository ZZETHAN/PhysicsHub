Introduction: The Invisible Force

Think about these questions:
  • Why doesn't the Moon fall down to Earth?
  • How do satellites stay in orbit without engines?
  • Why do you weigh less on the Moon than on Earth?
  • How fast must a rocket travel to escape Earth's gravity?

All these phenomena are governed by one of the fundamental forces of nature: gravity. The same force that makes an apple fall from a tree also keeps planets in orbit around the Sun.

Connection to Unit 12: In circular motion, we learned that centripetal force is required for circular motion. For planets and satellites, this force is provided by gravity. Combining these two concepts allows us to understand orbital mechanics.
Paper 4 Examination Focus: This unit frequently combines with Unit 12 (Circular Motion) in questions about satellite orbits. Expect calculations involving gravitational potential, orbital velocity, and escape velocity.

1. Newton's Law of Gravitation

The Key Question: Newton famously asked: "Does the same force that makes an apple fall also keep the Moon in orbit around Earth?"

Through brilliant insight and mathematical analysis, Newton discovered that every particle attracts every other particle with a force that follows a precise mathematical relationship.

1. Newton's Law of Gravitation

Any two point masses attract each other with a force that is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

\[F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}\]

where \(G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}\) (universal gravitational constant)

[Image Placeholder - Two particles gravitational attraction diagram]

1.1 Understanding the Inverse-Square Law

The \(\frac{1}{r^2}\) relationship is crucial. It means:

  • If distance doubles, force becomes \(\frac{1}{4}\)
  • If distance triples, force becomes \(\frac{1}{9}\)
  • If distance halves, force becomes \(4\) times stronger

1.2 The Gravitational Constant G

The value \(G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2 \text{ kg}^{-2}\) was first accurately measured by Henry Cavendish in 1789, more than 100 years after Newton proposed the law of gravitation.

The Cavendish Experiment: Using a delicate torsion balance, Cavendish measured the tiny gravitational force between two lead spheres in a laboratory. The apparatus consisted of:

  • Two small lead spheres attached to a T-shaped frame suspended by a thin wire
  • Two large lead spheres that could be moved close to the small spheres
  • A mirror attached to the wire to detect tiny twists using a light beam
  • The gravitational attraction between masses caused a measurable twist in the wire

Significance: This experiment:

  • Provided the first laboratory measurement of gravitational force between masses
  • Allowed calculation of Earth's mass for the first time (the "weighing the Earth" experiment)
  • Gave practical value to Newton's law for astronomical calculations
  • Confirmed that gravity acts between any two masses, not just celestial bodies
Common Exam Mistake: Students often forget that \(r\) is the distance between centers of mass, not surfaces. For two spheres, measure from center to center.

2. Gravitational Field Strength

The Key Question: Why do astronauts weigh less on the Moon? Is it because there's "no gravity" in space?

The answer lies in understanding that gravitational field strength varies with distance from a massive object. We define:

Definition: Gravitational Field Strength (g)

The gravitational force exerted per unit mass on a small object placed at that point.

\[g = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{GM}{r^2}\]

Unit: N kg⁻¹ or m s⁻²

Critical Insight: Notice that \(g\) is independent of the test mass. This means all objects fall with the same acceleration at a given location (Galileo's observation).

2.1 Variation with Distance

At Earth's surface: \(g_0 \approx 9.81 \text{ m s}^{-2}\)

At height \(h\) above surface: \(g = \frac{GM}{(R + h)^2}\)

3. Gravitational Potential

The Key Question: How much work is needed to move a mass from Earth's surface to infinity? And why is this concept useful?

Gravitational potential helps us calculate energy changes in gravitational fields without doing integration every time.

Definition: Gravitational Potential (φ)

The work done in bringing unit mass from infinity to the point.

\[\phi = -\frac{GM}{r}\]

Unit: J kg⁻¹ | Negative by convention (zero at infinity)

3.1 Why Negative?



The negative sign indicates that:

  • Work must be done by the field to bring a mass from infinity (attraction)
  • Work must be done against the field to move a mass to infinity
  • 3. Potential increases (becomes less negative) as \(r\) increases

[Image Placeholder - Graph of potential φ vs distance]

3.2 The Gravitational Potential Well

The concept of gravitational potential helps us visualize how objects move in gravitational fields. Imagine a spacecraft approaching Earth from a distant star:

  • At infinity: \(\phi = 0\) (our reference point), the spacecraft has maximum potential energy
  • As it approaches Earth: It is attracted by Earth's gravity, losing potential energy (becoming more negative)
  • At Earth's surface: Deepest in the "potential well" with \(\phi = -\frac{GM}{R}\)

The Potential Well Analogy: Think of a ball rolling into a bowl. At the rim (infinity), it has highest potential. Rolling down, it loses potential energy and gains kinetic energy. At the bottom (Earth's surface), it's at lowest potential and hardest to escape.

Different Bodies, Different Wells: The Sun creates a much deeper well than Earth due to its greater mass. Jupiter's well is intermediate. We live at the bottom of Earth's gravitational potential well.

3.3 Gravitational Potential Difference

The energy needed to move a mass between two points in a gravitational field depends on the potential difference:

\[\Delta E_p = m \Delta \phi = m(\phi_2 - \phi_1)\]

Practical Application: This is crucial for calculating the energy required to lift a satellite from Earth's surface to orbit, the work done by gravity as an object falls, and the escape energy needed to leave a planet's gravitational field.

Key Point: Since potential is always negative, moving to a higher (less negative) potential requires positive work by an external force, while falling to a lower (more negative) potential releases energy.

4. Orbital Motion

The Key Question: How do satellites stay in orbit? Why don't they fall back to Earth?

The answer combines Newton's law of gravitation with circular motion. For a satellite in circular orbit:

\[\text{Gravitational force} = \text{Centripetal force}\] \[\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}\]

Solving for orbital velocity:

\[v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}\]

4.1 Kepler's Third Law

The period of orbit can be derived:

\[T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM}}\]

Squaring both sides gives Kepler's Third Law:

\[T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}r^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad T^2 \propto r^3\]
Key Insight: Satellites closer to Earth (smaller \(r\)) have:
  • Higher orbital speed \(v\)
  • Shorter period \(T\)
  • Greater centripetal acceleration

5. Escape Velocity

The Key Question: How fast must a rocket travel to completely escape Earth's gravity and never return?

Using conservation of energy, we equate kinetic energy at launch to gravitational potential energy at infinity:

\[\frac{1}{2}mv_{escape}^2 + \left(-\frac{GMm}{r}\right) = 0 + 0\]

Solving for escape velocity:

\[v_{escape} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}} = \sqrt{2gr}\]

For Earth: \(v_{escape} \approx 11.2 \text{ km s}^{-1}\)

Key Relationship: Escape velocity is exactly \(\sqrt{2}\) times the orbital velocity at that radius: \[v_{escape} = \sqrt{2} \times v_{orbit}\]
