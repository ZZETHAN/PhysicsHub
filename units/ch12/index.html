Introduction: Real-World Questions

Think about these experiences:
  • 🎢 Why don't you fall out at the top of a roller coaster loop?
  • 🚗 When a car turns sharply, why do you feel pushed sideways?
  • 🛰️ How do satellites stay in orbit without falling back to Earth?
  • 🏍️ Why do motorcyclists lean into turns?

All these phenomena share one fundamental principle: an object moving in a circle is accelerating, even at constant speed. Let's discover why.

1. Angular Motion

The Key Question: Consider a spinning DVD. A point near the edge moves much faster than a point near the center. Yet, what do they have in common?

The Answer: Both points sweep through the same angle in the same time. This leads us to angular velocity.

Definition: Angular Velocity (ω)

The rate of change of angular displacement with respect to time.

\[\omega = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t} = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f\]

Unit: rad s⁻¹ | Must memorize: Period \(T\) and frequency \(f\) relationship

Connecting Angular and Linear: A point at distance \(r\) from the center:
\[v = r\omega\]

Important: All points have same \(\omega\), but \(v\) increases with \(r\).

2. Centripetal Acceleration

Critical Insight: Velocity is a vector (has magnitude AND direction). In circular motion, the direction of velocity is constantly changing. Any change in velocity means acceleration!

2.1 Vector Analysis: Finding the Acceleration Direction

Consider velocity vectors at two nearby points A and B on the circle:

  • \(\mathbf{v}_A\) is tangent at point A
  • \(\mathbf{v}_B\) is tangent at point B
  • Change in velocity: \(\Delta\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}_B - \mathbf{v}_A\)
Key Discovery

For small angles, \(\Delta\mathbf{v}\) points towards the centre of the circle. This is the direction of acceleration!

Definition: Centripetal Acceleration

The acceleration directed towards the centre of a circular path, caused by the continuous change in velocity direction.

\[a = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2 = v\omega\]

Direction: Always towards the centre

Common Exam Mistake: Students often think no acceleration exists at constant speed. Remember: Changing direction >>> means changing velocity >>> means acceleration!

3. Centripetal Force

Critical Understanding: Centripetal force is NOT a new type of force like gravity or friction. It is the resultant force (net force) directed towards the centre that causes circular motion. Always identify the real physical force(s) providing it.
Definition: Centripetal Force

The resultant force towards the centre of the circular path that produces centripetal acceleration.

\[F_{net} = ma = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2\]

4. Vertical Circular Motion

Vertical circular motion is more complex because gravity acts along the motion direction. The normal reaction varies throughout the motion.

4.1 At the Highest Point

Forces acting on the object (assuming moving clockwise):

  • Weight: \(mg\) (downwards)
  • Normal reaction: \(R\) (downwards, from track/string)
\[mg + R = \frac{mv^2}{r}\]
Minimum Speed at the Top (Critical Condition)

For the object to just complete the circle, \(R = 0\) at the top:

\[v_{min} = \sqrt{gr}\]

Must memorize: This is the minimum speed to maintain contact at the top.

4.2 At the Lowest Point

\[R - mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}\]

Here \(R\) is maximum. You feel heaviest at the bottom of a roller coaster!

Common Structured Question Pattern (4-6 marks):

"Explain why an object moving with constant speed in a circle is accelerating."

Model Answer:
  1. Velocity is a vector quantity with both magnitude and direction
  2. In circular motion, the direction of velocity is continuously changing
  3. Any change in velocity (direction or magnitude) means acceleration exists
  4. This acceleration is directed towards the centre of the circle
  5. Called centripetal acceleration, given by \(a = \frac{v^2}{r}\)
