Introduction: What is Quantum Physics?

What is Quantum Physics?
Quantum physics describes the behavior of matter and energy at the atomic and subatomic scale, where classical physics breaks down and new, counterintuitive principles emerge.

Key phenomena studied in this unit include:

  • Black body radiation — why hot objects glow
  • Photoelectric effect — how light ejects electrons from metals
  • Wave-particle duality — matter behaving as both waves and particles
  • Energy levels and line spectra — why atoms emit specific colors

1. Energy and Momentum of a Photon

1.1 Black Body Radiation

Definition — Black Body:
An object that absorbs all radiation incident on it. It appears black at room temperature and glows with characteristic color at high temperatures.
[Image Placeholder]
图 22.1.1: Black Body Radiation Intensity vs Wavelength
Peak shifts to shorter wavelengths as T increases | Hot objects glow from red → orange → white

Key Observations:

  1. Total radiation intensity increases with temperature
  2. Peak wavelength shifts to shorter wavelengths as T increases
  3. Hot objects glow from red → orange → white (shifts through the spectrum)
⭐ Classical Problem — Ultraviolet Catastrophe:
Classical physics predicted infinite energy at short wavelengths. This was wrong. Quantum theory solved this problem.

1.2 Planck's Quantum Hypothesis

Planck's Key Assumption:
Energy of vibrating particles is quantized — only discrete values are allowed.
$E = nhf \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, ...)$

Where $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s (Planck's constant)

Classical Physics Quantum Physics
Energy is continuous Energy is discrete (quantized)
Any value allowed Only multiples of $hf$

1.3 Einstein's Light Quanta

Einstein's Revolutionary Idea:
Light exists as discrete packets called photons (quanta).

Energy of one photon: $E = hf$

Key Properties of Photons:

  • Particle-like packets of electromagnetic energy
  • Massless (rest mass = 0)
  • Travel at speed of light $c$
  • Discrete, indivisible ("quanta")
Photon Energy Formula:
$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$

Where:

  • $h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s (Planck constant)
  • $f$ = frequency (Hz)
  • $\lambda$ = wavelength (m)
  • $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s (speed of light)
⭐ Worked Examples:

Red light (λ = 650 nm):
$E = hc/\lambda = (6.63 \times 10^{-34} \times 3 \times 10^8) / (650 \times 10^{-9})$
$E \approx 3.1 \times 10^{-19}$ J = 1.9 eV

Blue light (λ = 450 nm):
$E \approx 4.4 \times 10^{-19}$ J = 2.8 eV

1.4 Electron Volt

Definition — Electron Volt:
$1 \text{ eV} = \text{energy gained by an electron accelerated through 1 V} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J}$
$1 \text{ keV} = 10^3 \text{ eV}$
$1 \text{ MeV} = 10^6 \text{ eV}$

1.5 Photon Momentum

$p = \frac{E}{c} = \frac{hf}{c} = \frac{h}{\lambda}$
Evidence for Photon Momentum:
Comet tails always point away from the Sun due to radiation pressure — photons carry momentum!

2. Photoelectric Effect

Definition — Photoelectric Effect:
Emission of electrons from a metal surface when electromagnetic radiation above a threshold frequency shines on it.

图 22.2.1: Photoelectric Effect Experiment
UV light → Zinc plate → Electron emission → Electroscope deflects

2.1 Key Observations

Experiment 1: Frequency Variation

Observation Wave Theory Prediction Photon Theory Reality
No emission below threshold $f$ Energy accumulates Photon energy insufficient
Instant emission above threshold Delay expected One-to-one photon-electron
Higher frequency = more energetic $e^-$ Wrong $KE_{max} = hf - \Phi$

Experiment 2: Intensity Variation

Change Effect on Electrons
Higher intensity More electrons emitted (more photons)
Same intensity, higher frequency Same number, but more energetic

2.2 Threshold Frequency and Work Function

Work Function ($\Phi$):
Minimum energy needed to liberate an electron from a metal surface.
$f_0 = \frac{\Phi}{h}$    (threshold frequency)
Metal Work Function (eV) Threshold λ
Sodium 1.9 eV ~650 nm (red)
Zinc 4.3 eV ~290 nm (UV)
Copper 4.4 eV ~280 nm (UV)

2.3 Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

$hf = \Phi + KE_{max}$

Or:

$KE_{max} = hf - \Phi = \frac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2$

Key Predictions:

  1. $KE_{max}$ depends only on frequency (not intensity)
  2. Threshold frequency exists — below it, no emission
  3. Emission is instantaneous (one-to-one interaction)
  4. Higher intensity → more electrons, same max energy
[Image Placeholder]
图 22.2.2: Photoelectric Energy Diagram
Photon energy hf | Work function Φ (binding energy) | K.E.max = hf - Φ (excess energy)
⭐ Exam Tip — Always Check:
  • $hf > \Phi$ for electron emission to occur
  • $KE_{max} = hf - \Phi$ (not just hf)
  • Higher frequency → more energetic electrons, not more electrons

3. Wave-Particle Duality

3.1 de Broglie Wavelength

de Broglie's Hypothesis (1924):
All matter has wave-like properties.

Wavelength: $\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$
For electrons accelerated by voltage V:
$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e KE}} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e eV}}$
⭐ Worked Example:
An electron accelerated through 100 V:
$KE = eV = 100$ eV $= 1.6 \times 10^{-17}$ J
$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m_e KE}} = \frac{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}{\sqrt{2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-17}}}$
$\lambda \approx 1.2 \times 10^{-10}$ m = 0.12 nm (comparable to atomic spacing!)

3.3 Complementary Phenomena

Light Phenomenon Evidence
Wave nature Interference, diffraction Double-slit, diffraction gratings
Particle nature Photoelectric effect Single photon interactions
Electrons Phenomenon Evidence
Particle nature Deflection in magnetic fields $F = Bqv$
Wave nature Electron diffraction Diffraction patterns from crystals
Complementarity Principle:
Nature is complementary — different experiments reveal different aspects. Both wave and particle descriptions are needed; neither alone is complete.

4. Energy Levels and Line Spectra

4.1 Energy Levels in Atoms

Key Idea: Electrons in atoms can only occupy discrete energy levels — not continuous values.
[Image Placeholder]
图 22.4.1: Energy Level Diagram
E₃ ——— | E₂ ——— | E₁ —— | E₀ ═══ Ground state
  • Ground State: Lowest energy (electron bound)
  • Excited State: Higher energy level
  • Ionization: Electron completely removed (E = 0)

Hydrogen Energy Levels (eV):

Level Energy
n = 1 (ground) −13.6 eV
n = 2 −3.4 eV
n = 3 −1.51 eV
n = ∞ (ionized) 0 eV
Hydrogen Energy Formula:
$E_n = -\frac{13.6}{n^2}$ eV

4.2 Line Spectra

[Image Placeholder]
图 22.4.2: Emission vs Absorption Spectra
Emission: Bright lines on dark background | Absorption: Dark lines on bright background

Emission Spectrum: When electrons fall from higher to lower energy levels, photons are emitted at specific frequencies.

Absorption Spectrum: When white light passes through a gas, photons are absorbed at specific frequencies, causing dark lines.

Photon Energy for Transition:
$\Delta E = hf = E_f - E_i$
Conservation of Energy:
  • Emission: Electron falls → photon emitted
  • Absorption: Photon absorbed → electron excited
